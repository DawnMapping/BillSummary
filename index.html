The market problem

What 73,000 businesses are being sold right now

The AML compliance market that formed around Tranche 2 was designed to profit from uncertainty.

😱

Fear-based messaging

"You'll face $33M fines" repeated on every competitor page. The threat is real but the implication — that you need an expensive consultant to survive it — is not. Most newly regulated businesses have manageable, low-risk profiles.

📄

Document delivery, not decision systems

Most providers produce a policy document. Your obligation isn't a document — it's a functioning program: decisions made, processes followed, records kept. A policy no one reads is not compliance.

🌏

Offshore firms, no local context

Multiple firms dominating Google rankings for "AML consultant Australia" are based in India or New Zealand. No Australian government experience, no knowledge of how AUSTRAC actually operates. See Competitors tab for specifics.

💸

Pricing not calibrated to the obligation

$10K–$50K+ for implementation and $23K/year ongoing. None of this is driven by what the law requires — it's driven by market anxiety. A solo conveyancer and a major law firm do not need the same program.

The actual standard: AUSTRAC requires a risk-based AML/CTF program proportionate to your business. For a small professional services firm with low-risk clients, that is achievable and not expensive — if built correctly.
The different approach

Build something defensible. Operate it. Get on with your business.

You don't need to do the cops' job for them. Your obligation under the AML/CTF Act is to know your customer, monitor for suspicious activity, report what you observe, and document your reasoning. Build a system that meets that legal standard, operate it consistently, and get back to what you do.

The difference between a defensible compliance system and an expensive one is understanding precisely where the legal standard sits — and building to that, not to the fear around it.

    The ACAMS Ecosystem — the dominant AML certification body

    Most competitors cite ACAMS credentials. Here's what they actually represent:

    🏛
    TIER 1 — EARNED, NOT PURCHASED
    Government & Regulatory Experience
    Direct experience with AUSTRAC, AGD, ACIC, AFP, or ASIC. Intelligence roles, law enforcement, policy work on the AML/CTF Act itself. Knows how the regulator thinks, what they investigate, and how enforcement decisions are made.
    Competitors with this
    0 of 4
    advisory firms reviewed
    🎓
    Tier 2 — Rare
    Academic / Published
    Peer-reviewed publications in law/finance journals. Doctoral research in financial crime. Requires years of original work with external validation. Very few practitioners have this.
    Oxford UP publication or equivalent = credible signal
    📜
    Tier 3 — Common
    CAMS (ACAMS)
    ~6 hours of study. Multiple-choice exam. $1,295 USD. Held by ~100,000 people globally. Demonstrates familiarity with AML principles — not Australian regulatory knowledge, not enforcement insight.
    A Mumbai banking officer can hold CAMS. So can most competitors.
    🔍
    Tier 4 — Padding
    FCA / ACA / ACS / CISA
    Indian or international accounting/auditing designations (ICAI, ICSI, ISACA). Relevant to financial controls generally — not specifically to Australian AML/CTF law. Commonly listed alongside CAMS to bulk out credential sections.
    These are accounting qualifications, not AML expertise.
    Also seen:
    CGSS (ACAMS governance add-on) CAMS-Audit CAMS-RM → These are supplementary ACAMS exams. Still commercial certifications, not regulatory experience.

    The technology layer — a different type of competitor

    Software products targeting the same clients

    These aren't advisory firms — they're tech products. But they're being sold as compliance solutions to the same 73,000 businesses. The critical distinction: software handles document management and identity checks. It cannot write a risk assessment, train staff, advise on when to file an SMR, or represent you if AUSTRAC comes knocking.

    🏠
    PEXA Group / PEXA Clear
    pexa.com.au · ASX-listed · Property settlement infrastructure + CDD tool + AML training
    CDD tool, not a compliance program Now selling AML training to conveyancers Deep property sector reach

    PEXA is Australia's dominant digital property settlement platform — used in most property transactions in VIC, NSW, QLD, WA, SA.     PEXA AML training is a newer product: short-form online courses aimed at conveyancers and real estate agents. Positioned as helping meet the staff training obligation under Tranche 2. Priced accessibly, rolled out through existing PEXA relationships.

    Other software tools in the market

    AMLGuard

    $45–$90+/mo
    SaaSPer-client pricing

    Program builder and customer screening tool. Individual client $45, high-risk $60, full entity $90+/month. Targets Tranche 2 directly. Provides document templates and screening — not professional advisory judgment.

    Gap: Can generate documents but can't tell you if your risk assessment actually reflects your business reality.

    easyAML

    $49/month
    SaaSTemplate-based

    Template-based AML/CTF program builder. DIY model — answer questions, generate a program document. Price point makes it attractive to micro-businesses. Program output is generic by design.

    Gap: Template programs are not risk-assessed to your actual client base. AUSTRAC expects proportionality to your specific business.

    FreeAML

    $15–$35/month
    SaaSEntry price

    Lowest price point in the market. Individual tier $15, entity $35. Draws price-sensitive clients. Minimal advisory functionality — essentially a CDD record-keeping tool with a compliance checklist overlay.

    Gap: Price drives selection here, not capability. A $15/month tool will not give you a defensible program if AUSTRAC investigates.

    Law Society / Peak Body Toolkits

    Free–low cost
    Association resources

    Law Council, CAANZ, CPA Australia, REIA, AIC are all developing Tranche 2 toolkits for members — guidance notes, template policies, webinar recordings. Free or included in membership. Generic but credible for orientation.

    Gap: Association toolkits give sector guidance — not your specific business's risk-based program. They explicitly disclaim legal advice.
    The layered market: Software (PEXA Clear, AMLGuard, easyAML) handles CDD checks and document management. Peak body toolkits give sector orientation. Neither provides what AUSTRAC actually requires: a properly risk-assessed, implemented program with operational staff training and SMR decision frameworks. That's the advisory layer — and it's the one that's genuinely underserved for the 69% of newly regulated businesses with 4 or fewer employees.
